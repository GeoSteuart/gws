# GWI Research Evaluation Protocol — Veteran Edition
## Ford Research Framework, Version 2.0

---

### What This Protocol Does

Someone showed you a study about Gulf War Illness. Maybe it was shared in a Facebook group, sent by a friend, or cited to support a claim about a treatment or a cause. This protocol gives you a framework to evaluate whether that study is worth believing — and how much weight to put on it.

You do not need a medical degree to use this. You need to be able to read a study and answer honest questions about it.

---

### The Five Things That Matter Most

Before anything else, ask these five questions. If you get a red flag on any of them, be skeptical of whatever the study is being used to prove.

**1. Is someone selling something?**
If the study is being used to promote a supplement, treatment, program, or book with a price tag attached, treat every claim from it with strong skepticism. Research that exists to sell a product is not independent research.

**2. Does the claim match what the study actually tested?**
A study that tested blood pressure in 40 veterans cannot prove that a diet cures Gulf War Illness. A study that found a protein in 15 veterans' blood cannot prove that protein causes all GWI symptoms in all veterans. Good science makes narrow claims. Watch for anyone stretching a study's findings beyond what it actually measured.

**3. Where did it come from?**
University hospitals, VA research centers, and National Institutes of Health-funded programs are credible sources. Private institutes you have never heard of, journals that publish anything for a fee, and book chapters from unknown publishers are warning signs.

**4. Do other independent research teams agree?**
One study by one team proves very little. The same finding from multiple independent research groups, using different methods, in different veteran populations, is much more convincing. Ask whether other researchers have tested the same claim and found the same thing.

**5. How do the researchers describe their own findings?**
Careful researchers use cautious language: "suggests," "may indicate," "further research needed," "preliminary finding." Researchers who claim to have definitively proven something major, without qualification, are either overconfident or overselling. Honest science acknowledges what it does not know.

### What the Ratings Mean

Every study evaluated using this protocol receives a quality rating of A, B, C, or D. Here is what each rating means in plain language.

**Level A — High Quality**
The study was designed carefully, used objective measurements where possible, had an appropriate comparison group, was large enough to support its conclusions, and was funded independently of any party with a financial stake in the outcome. Findings from Level A studies carry real weight. They can form the basis of decisions about treatment or policy.

**Level B — Good Quality**
The study was well-designed with some limitations that the researchers acknowledged honestly. The findings are reliable for their specific claims. A Level B study is worth taking seriously, but its conclusions should be understood within the constraints of those limitations. Most good GWI research falls in this category.

**Level C — Limited Quality**
The study has real methodological problems — small sample, unreliable exposure information, weak comparison group, or self-reported outcomes without validation. A Level C study raises a question or points in a direction but should not be the only reason to change your mind about something. Treat it as a starting point that needs confirmation from stronger research.

**Level D — Poor Quality / Do Not Use**
The study has fundamental flaws that prevent it from answering the question it claims to address. This includes no comparison group when one was needed, commercial conflicts of interest, non-validated outcomes, or contaminated data. Level D studies should not be cited as evidence of anything except methodological failure. If someone uses a Level D study to convince you of something, that is a red flag about their judgment, not a reason to update your beliefs.

---

### What the Tags Mean

Each section of a study evaluation carries a tag. Here is what each tag means.

**PASS** — This dimension of the study is solid. No material concern identified.

**CAUTION** — There is a real limitation here that affects how much weight to put on the finding. The limitation is acknowledged and does not necessarily invalidate the study, but it should be understood before accepting the conclusion.

**FLAG** — This is a serious problem. It means something is wrong with the study in this dimension that undermines the finding. Pay close attention to what is flagged.

**NOTE** — Context worth knowing. Not a problem, but information that shapes how to interpret the finding — for example, who funded the study or how this study relates to other research.

**N/A** — This dimension does not apply to this type of study. For example, a lab test on blood samples does not have a battlefield exposure assessment, and that is not a flaw — it just means this section is not relevant.

---

### What the Confidence Levels Mean

When multiple studies are evaluated together, the body of evidence is assigned a confidence level from C1 to C5.

**C5 — High confidence.** Multiple well-designed studies from independent research groups all point to the same conclusion. This is the standard required before a finding should influence clinical practice or policy.

**C4 — Moderate-to-high confidence.** Generally consistent evidence with minor limitations across studies. The finding is reliable but some uncertainty remains.

**C3 — Moderate confidence.** Findings are generally consistent but the research has real methodological limitations. Worth taking seriously while waiting for stronger evidence.

**C2 — Limited confidence.** Studies are inconsistent, small, or methodologically compromised. A question is raised but not answered.

**C1 — Insufficient evidence.** Not enough reliable research exists to draw a conclusion. This does not mean the answer is no — it means the question has not been properly studied yet.

### A Note on GWI Research Specifically

Gulf War Illness research has a specific problem that affects almost every study in the field: the records were not kept properly. Many veterans were not told what chemicals they were exposed to. Vaccination records were sometimes deliberately not documented. The government reclassified which units were considered exposed between 1997 and 2000, removing 152 Army units from the exposed list, which affected every study that used those official lists to define who was exposed and who was not.

This does not mean GWI research is worthless. It means that self-reported exposure data, small sample sizes, and studies that relied on government exposure classifications without questioning them should all be read with awareness of this structural problem. The best GWI research uses biological measurements — blood tests, genetic analysis, brain scans — that do not depend on anyone's memory or on government records.

When a study's findings come from a blood test or a genetic analysis, memory problems cannot explain the result. When a study's findings come entirely from asking veterans to remember what happened 30 years ago, those problems matter.

---

### Part I: Evidence Classification

Studies are classified by design. Level I is a randomized controlled trial or a systematic review of multiple trials — the strongest design. Level II is a study that compares groups of people over time, such as comparing veterans who developed GWI against veterans who did not. Level III is a survey, a snapshot study, or a series of cases. Level IV is individual case reports or anecdotal accounts. Level V is expert opinion or a summary of existing research without new data.

Higher level does not always mean better for every question. A well-executed Level III study with biological measurements can provide stronger evidence on a specific mechanistic question than a poorly designed Level II epidemiological study. The level tells you the design; the rating tells you the quality within that design.

---

### Part II: Exposure Assessment

For studies examining whether a particular Gulf War exposure caused illness, this section evaluates how exposure was determined. The key questions are: did the study use military records, self-report, or the government's official exposure classifications? Did it acknowledge the limitations of whichever approach it used? Did it account for the sequential nature of exposures — specifically that pyridostigmine bromide pills were discontinued before chemical agent exposure at Khamisiyah, creating a vulnerability window that simultaneous-exposure models miss entirely?

For laboratory studies, brain scans, and genetic analyses that do not examine exposure-outcome relationships, this section does not apply.

---

### Part III: Comparison Group

A study that only looks at sick veterans without comparing them to a group of healthy veterans, or healthy veterans without comparing them to sick veterans, cannot establish a relationship between any factor and illness. This section evaluates whether the comparison group was appropriately constructed.

The specific GWI problem here is that studies using government exposure lists to define the unexposed comparison group may have accidentally included exposed veterans in that group — because the government's reclassification in 2000 removed 152 exposed Army units from the exposed list. A study whose unexposed comparison group actually contains exposed veterans will find smaller effects than actually exist.

---

### Part IV: Recall Bias

Veterans are being asked to remember events from more than 30 years ago, often while suffering from a serious illness. Research has documented a specific pattern in GWI studies: veterans with ongoing illness tend to remember and report more exposures than veterans who stayed healthy. Veterans who stayed healthy tend to underreport exposures they actually had. Both effects happen at the same time in the same dataset, which means the overall direction of distortion cannot be calculated from the study itself.

When a study's primary findings come from blood tests, genetic analysis, brain imaging, or other objective biological measurements, this problem does not apply. Those results do not depend on anyone's memory.

---

### Part V: Sample Size

Bigger studies are generally more reliable than smaller studies, but size is not everything. A small study whose results show a highly specific pattern — for example, a consistent protective antibody effect across every individual subject tested, or selective results that differ across multiple carefully controlled conditions — can provide strong internal evidence even with a small number of human subjects. However, a small study cannot tell you whether the finding applies to all GWI veterans, because the people studied may not represent the broader population.

This protocol requires that both be stated clearly: what the study shows internally, and what can and cannot be concluded about all veterans based on the sample size. A small, well-done study is valuable preliminary evidence. It is not a population-level conclusion.

---

### Part VI: Methodological Quality

This section evaluates whether the study was well-designed and well-reported: were the methods described clearly enough that another researcher could repeat the study; were the limitations acknowledged honestly; was the funding source disclosed. Two standard checklists — CONSORT for clinical trials and STROBE for observational studies — are used as reference frameworks where they fit the study design.

---

### Part VII: Who Paid For It

Research funded by a party with a financial or institutional interest in the outcome requires additional scrutiny. This does not automatically mean the research is wrong, but it means the incentive structure should be understood. Studies funded by veterans' foundations, universities, or government health research agencies without a direct stake in the finding are more independent than studies funded by companies selling related products.

---

### Part VIII: Do Other Studies Agree

A single study finding is a signal, not a conclusion. This section evaluates whether findings from other research groups, using different methods, in different veteran populations, point in the same direction. When they do, the conclusion becomes substantially stronger. When they do not, the finding remains preliminary regardless of its internal quality.

An important distinction: a study can be theoretically consistent with an existing framework without confirming that framework. Consistency is context. Confirmation requires direct measurement or independent replication.

---

### Part IX: Summary Rating

The evaluation ends with a plain statement of what the study shows, what it does not show, the quality rating, and how this study should be used when thinking about the broader evidence base.

---

### Part X: Putting Studies Together

When evaluating a body of research rather than a single study, confidence levels from C1 to C5 apply to the overall conclusion. Two specific rules matter most for GWI research:

When a study contains both a narrow finding and a broader claim built on that finding, the narrow finding and the broader claim are rated separately. A study that found a protein in 15 veterans' blood is a strong narrow finding (the protein is there). The broader claim — that this protein causes GWI in all veterans — requires much more evidence. Both can be stated, but they carry different confidence levels.

The most fundamental gap in all of GWI research is that no prospective study was conducted. No one collected baseline data on veterans before they deployed, documented their exposures precisely as they happened, measured biological outcomes over time, and followed the cohort forward. That study cannot be done now, 35 years later. Every GWI study is working around this absence. The best research uses biological measurements to partially compensate for it. The worst research does not acknowledge it at all.
